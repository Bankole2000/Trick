# TRICK GAME: MINIMAL PLAYABLE PROTOTYPE (v0.1)

## Overview

TRICK is a communication and truth-evaluation card game built on the idea that language can be modeled as a game in which statements are exchanged, interpreted, challenged, accepted, rejected, and ultimately compared against reality.

The game is designed to explore:

* Truth
* Belief
* Interpretation
* Misunderstanding
* Communication

while remaining simple enough to be played as a social card game.

---

# Design Goals

The prototype aims to:

1. Create a fun and fast social game.
2. Encourage critical thinking.
3. Demonstrate how communication can succeed or fail.
4. Provide a foundation for more advanced game variants.

The deeper R–B–U–I–L model exists beneath the game but is not required knowledge for players.

---

# Components

## Statement Deck

A deck of cards containing statements.

Examples:

* Water boils at 100°C at sea level.
* The Earth is flat.
* Humans only use 10% of their brains.
* Money has intrinsic value.

Each card contains:

### Front

* Statement

### Back (Hidden)

* Truth value (Reality, R)
* Explanation
* Category (optional)

---

## Tokens (Optional)

Players may use:

* Score Tokens
* Challenge Tokens
* Misunderstanding Tokens

Paper and pencil may also be used.

# Number of Players

Recommended:

* 2–6 players

Possible:

* Solo mode
* Team mode

# Core Concepts

## Statement

A proposition presented to players.

Example:

> "The Sun is a star."

---

## Speaker

The player whose turn it is.

The speaker chooses how to present the statement.

---

## Listener

All other players.

Listeners evaluate the statement and the speaker.

---

## Challenge

A listener may request verification of the statement.

Challenges are the primary mechanism for testing certainty and detecting deception.

---

# Game Setup

1. Shuffle deck.
2. Determine first speaker.
3. Give each player a score counter starting at 0.
4. Place deck in center of table.

---

# Round Structure

Each round consists of five phases.

---

## Phase 1: Draw

Draw one card.

The statement is revealed to all players.

Example:

> "Lightning never strikes the same place twice."

The truth value remains hidden.

---

## Phase 2: Speaker Action

The speaker chooses one of the following actions.

### Assert

The speaker claims:

> "This statement is true."

---

### Negate

The speaker claims:

> "This statement is false."

---

### Doubt

The speaker claims:

> "I am unsure."

This option may receive special scoring bonuses.

---

## Phase 3: Listener Response

All listeners secretly choose one of the following.

### Accept

The listener agrees with the speaker.

---

### Reject

The listener disagrees with the speaker.

---

### Question

The listener requests additional reasoning.

---

### Challenge

The listener demands verification.

If any challenge occurs, the game proceeds immediately to Resolution.

---

## Phase 4: Resolution

The card is flipped.

Reality (R) is revealed.

The explanation is read aloud.

Example:

Statement:

> "Humans only use 10% of their brains."

Reality:

> False.

Explanation:

> Modern neuroscience shows most regions of the brain are active at different times.

---

## Phase 5: Scoring

Points are awarded.

---

# Scoring System

## Truth Score

Correct evaluation:

+2 points

Incorrect evaluation:

-2 points

---

## Challenge Score

Successful challenge:

+2 points

Failed challenge:

-1 point

---

## Doubt Bonus

If a player selected Doubt and the statement is especially difficult or ambiguous:

+1 point

(Optional Rule)

---

## Alignment Bonus

If all players converge on the correct answer:

+1 point to all players

This rewards shared understanding.

---

# End of Game

After a predetermined number of rounds:

Recommended:

* 10 rounds
* 15 rounds
* 20 rounds

The player with the highest score wins.

---

# Optional Cooperative Mode

Instead of competing, players attempt to maximize group understanding.

Group Goals:

* Maximize Truth Score
* Minimize Misunderstanding
* Achieve consensus where appropriate

Win if the group exceeds a target score.

# Prototype Categories

The starter deck may contain several categories.

## Anchor Cards

Clear factual statements.

Purpose:

* Ground reality.
* Build confidence.

Examples:

* Water boils at 100°C at sea level.
* The Sun is a star.

---

## Myth Cards

Common misconceptions.

Purpose:

* Reveal incorrect assumptions.

Examples:

* Humans only use 10% of their brains.
* Lightning never strikes the same place twice.

---

## Social Cards

Statements about social reality.

Purpose:

* Create discussion and interpretation differences.

Examples:

* Money has value because people agree it has value.
* Reputation affects outcomes.

---

## Mirror Cards

Meta-level statements about communication.

Purpose:

* Increase awareness of misunderstanding itself.

Examples:

* A statement can influence belief even when false.
* Two rational people can disagree.

---

# Hidden Engine

The game is based on a communication model called R–B–U–I–L.

R = Reality

B = Speaker Belief

U = Speaker Utterance

I = Listener Interpretation

L = Listener Belief

Communication can be represented as:

R → B → U → I → L

Misunderstandings occur when alignment breaks between layers.

Future versions of TRICK may expose these layers directly through advanced rules.

---

# Design Philosophy

TRICK is based on the idea that communication is not merely the transfer of information.

Communication is a game in which:

* statements are moves,
* beliefs are states,
* questions are probes,
* challenges are tests,
* and understanding is achieved through alignment.

The objective is not simply to discover truth.

The objective is to understand how truth, belief, and interpretation interact.
